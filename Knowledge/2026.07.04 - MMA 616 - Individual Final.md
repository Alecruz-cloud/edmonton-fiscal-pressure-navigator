What you build
An agentic workspace: a Claude Code project, organized around a CLAUDE.md, that you can point at a
dataset to get real analytical work done. It is not, by default, an app with a user interface. It is the working
environment itself — the specification, the data connected as a live source, and the skills and subagents that
do the work — set up so that an agent can carry out a defined kind of analysis on your dataset reliably and
repeatedly.

A workspace has four parts, the same four the course is built around:

  The living spec (CLAUDE.md) — what the workspace is for: the analytical work it does, who it is
for, what good output looks like, what is out of scope, and the conventions the agent should follow.
  Knowledge — your Government of Alberta dataset connected as a live source (a file, tool, API, or
connector), with the supporting context the agent needs to read it well (a data dictionary, domain
notes), kept tight and current.

  Skills — the reusable capability you package so the work repeats rather than starting from a fresh
prompt each time: Claude Skills, subagents for scoped tasks, and workflows that string steps
together.

  Governance — where you review the agent’s work, what it decides on its own versus escalates to

you, and how you keep the output honest.

You may build a small interface or dashboard to demonstrate your work.

Your dataset and the work it does
Your dataset is a Government of Alberta open dataset, and it must be different from the data your group
used — a different dataset and a different problem. That difference is what makes the final your own work.
(See the end of these instructions for where to find one.)

Because the dataset comes first here, be deliberate about the work it does. A workspace is only worth building
if it does something a real person would want done. Decide who would use the analysis and what decision or
task it supports, then build the workspace to do that work well — not to expose every field the data happens
to contain. A clear user and a clear purpose are what separate a strong final from a tour of the dataset.

Putting the workspace to work
A workspace you have only configured is not finished. Use it: run it to produce the analyses, briefings, or
answers it was built for, against your real dataset. Keep a short log of what you ran, where the output was
good, where it fell short, what you corrected, and anything that surprised you. You are working alone and

Building your own evaluation
As in the group project, you test the output, not the code: are the results useful and accurate for the work the
workspace does? Assemble a small set of test cases paired with what a good answer should look like, and a
way to check the workspace’s output against them — recomputing the numbers independently, and reviewing
any narrative output yourself rather than having a model score it. Measure once, improve the workspace, and
measure again so you can show the difference. The score matters less than what it tells you about where the
output is weak and what you changed to fix it.

Working on your own
This is an individual project. You may use AI freely, and you may talk with classmates about approach, but
the workspace, the analysis, and the write-up must be your own. The distinctness rule — a different
Government of Alberta dataset and a different problem than your group’s — is the main safeguard, and the
distinctness statement is where you account for it. If you are unsure whether a dataset or problem is too close
to your group’s, ask before you build.

Finding a Government of Alberta dataset
Start at the Government of Alberta open data portal, which holds thousands of datasets. A good candidate
has many records, free text or several related tables, a published data dictionary, and a clear real user.
Domains worth browsing include labour and employment, health, agriculture, energy, the environment,
education, transportation, and public safety.

  Choose something different from your group’s dataset and problem. Avoid the datasets on the group

project menu — including the Alberta wildfire data — so your final is clearly your own.

  Datasets and links change; confirm availability before you commit to one.
  The instructor can help you scope a candidate dataset.
